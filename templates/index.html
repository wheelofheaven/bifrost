What if the gods of Genesis were a civilization?

Wheel of Heaven is a long reading of the ancient world's creation traditions through a single working hypothesis: that the beings the Hebrew Bible calls Elohim—a plural noun, masked by translation as a single God—were a small advanced human civilization who came here, prepared the planet, designed life, and left a record in the religious texts. The reading runs across twelve ages of the precessional cycle.

The Working Hypothesis

The gods of the older traditions appear, repeatedly, as a group.

The Hebrew Bible's word for the figures who created humanity is Elohim—a plural noun, masked by translation as the singular "God." Across the older creation traditions of the ancient world—Mesopotamian, biblical, Vedic, Mesoamerican, and others—the figures who shape the first humans appear repeatedly as a team, not a single deity, and as beings with technology and intentions that look strikingly like an organized expedition.

Wheel of Heaven takes this seriously as a working hypothesis: that the Elohim were an advanced human civilization from elsewhere, that what we have inherited as religion is the long memory of their work, and that the precessional cycle the ancient astronomers preserved is the calendar that records it. The site is built to test this reading against the available evidence—text by text, age by age—and to be checked.

The Cosmic Clock

Twelve ages. Twenty-six thousand years. One arc.

Earth's axis traces a slow circle in the sky—the precession of the equinoxes—completing one full cycle in roughly 25,920 years. Divided by the twelve constellations of the zodiac, that cycle yields ages of about 2,160 years each. Ancient astronomers from Mesopotamia to Mesoamerica encoded this clock in their monuments and myths. The Wheel of Heaven reading runs the events of the creation traditions across its twelve ages, treating each as a phase of one long project—from the project's first arrival in Capricorn twenty-two thousand years ago to the present age of Aquarius.
